  • Bio

My dog Benji died in 2001. I was seven and it devastated me. The same year, Scooby Doo and the Cyber Chase came out, the War on Terror began, and the Fiber-Optic Link Around the Globe plugged the US into an intercontinental network. It was the year of the Space Odyssey and the longest strike in NYC’s history, the year my brother and I got our first computer.

 Using all the words I had, I began to write a dystopic story on my computer. I built a world of survival, of repair, of keys and codes that fell apart in your hands but if rightly reconfigured, could open doors into new dimensions. It came out just as one might expect: a shoddy retelling of the Matrix sequel, the plot of which had been recounted to me by my father’s friend earlier that year.

Today, my work tries to live up to the story I believed I was writing. It was and is a fucked up world full of money-hungry governments, guarded borders, absent fathers and the like, and I do my best to make sense of it using ritualistic action and oblique logics. I build custom broadcasters and satellites so as to speculatively listen for a signal that oscillates between not-there & not-yet-there. This oscillation opens a hole; it is where Benji lives, it is where we dig. Flags, maps, and indexes are inverted into emotional vessels; biased and brimming. These pieces become tools in my search for the matrix as my nine-year-old self imagined: materialized, exploding, and closer than one might think.
